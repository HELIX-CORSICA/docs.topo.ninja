Topo.ninja's [Coordinate Converter](/en/tools/coordinate-converter) transforms coordinates between different Coordinate Reference Systems (CRS). This page explains the underlying concepts and how they are implemented.

## Context

A Coordinate Reference System (CRS) defines how coordinates on the Earth's surface are expressed as numbers. Different CRS exist for different purposes:

- **Geographic CRS** (e.g., WGS84): coordinates are latitude and longitude in degrees
- **Projected CRS** (e.g., Lambert 93, UTM): coordinates are Easting and Northing in meters on a flat map projection

Surveyors frequently need to convert between these systems -- for example, when GNSS observations in WGS84 need to be expressed in a national grid for plans and construction.

## Key concepts

### EPSG codes

Every CRS has a unique identifier in the EPSG registry. Common examples:

| EPSG code | Name | Type | Use |
|-----------|------|------|-----|
| 4326 | WGS84 | Geographic | GPS, global reference |
| 2154 | RGF93 / Lambert 93 | Projected | France national grid |
| 32631 | WGS84 / UTM zone 31N | Projected | Europe, North Africa |
| 3857 | Web Mercator | Projected | Web maps (Google, OSM) |

When you select a CRS in Topo.ninja, you are choosing an EPSG code that fully defines the datum, ellipsoid, and projection parameters.

### Geoid grids and height conversion

#### Ellipsoidal vs. orthometric heights

GNSS receivers measure **ellipsoidal height** $h$ -- the distance above the mathematical ellipsoid (e.g., GRS80 or WGS84). But surveyors and engineers work with **orthometric height** $H$ -- the height above mean sea level (the geoid).

The relationship between them is:

$$
H = h - N
$$

where $N$ is the **geoid undulation** -- the difference between the ellipsoid and the geoid at that location.

#### Geoid grid files

The geoid undulation $N$ varies from place to place and is provided by regional geoid models in grid files. Common examples:

| Grid | Region | Provided by |
|------|--------|-------------|
| RAF20 | France | IGN |
| EGM2008 | Global | NGA |
| OSGM15 | UK | Ordnance Survey |

When you enable a geoid grid in the Coordinate Converter, the tool applies the local $N$ value to convert between ellipsoidal and orthometric heights.

<Warning>
  Without a geoid grid, height conversions between ellipsoidal and orthometric systems can have errors of tens of meters. Always use the appropriate geoid grid when height accuracy matters.
</Warning>

### DMS parsing

Geographic coordinates can be expressed in several formats:

| Format | Example | Name |
|--------|---------|------|
| Decimal degrees | 48.856944 | DD |
| Degrees and decimal minutes | 48° 51.4167' | DM |
| Degrees, minutes, seconds | 48° 51' 24.5" | DMS |

Topo.ninja automatically parses all these formats. The internal representation is always decimal degrees, and conversion between formats follows:

$$
\text{DD} = d + \frac{m}{60} + \frac{s}{3600}
$$

where $d$ is degrees, $m$ is minutes, and $s$ is seconds.

The parser handles various notations: degree symbols (°), apostrophes ('), double quotes ("), cardinal suffixes (N, S, E, W), and spaces.

## Common transformations

### WGS84 to Lambert 93

This is the most common conversion for surveyors working in France. It involves:

1. **Datum alignment**: WGS84 and RGF93 are practically identical (sub-centimeter difference), so no datum shift is needed for most applications
2. **Map projection**: Lambert Conformal Conic with two standard parallels (44°N and 49°N), centered on the Paris meridian

$$
\text{WGS84 (lat, lon)} \xrightarrow{\text{projection}} \text{Lambert 93 (E, N)}
$$

### Lambert 93 to UTM

Both are projected CRS but use different projections:
- Lambert 93: Lambert Conformal Conic, covers all of France in one zone
- UTM: Transverse Mercator, 6-degree-wide zones

The transformation goes through the geographic intermediate:

$$
\text{Lambert 93 (E, N)} \xrightarrow{\text{inverse}} \text{RGF93 (lat, lon)} \xrightarrow{\text{projection}} \text{UTM (E, N)}
$$

### Geographic to Web Mercator

Web Mercator (EPSG:3857) is used by web maps (Google Maps, OpenStreetMap). It is a simplified Mercator projection that treats the Earth as a sphere:

$$
\text{WGS84 (lat, lon)} \xrightarrow{\text{spherical Mercator}} \text{EPSG:3857 (E, N)}
$$

<Info>
  Web Mercator is not suitable for precise surveying due to its spherical approximation. Use it only for visualization purposes.
</Info>

## Interpreting results

After conversion, verify your results make sense:

- **Latitude/Longitude** should be in a reasonable range for your region (e.g., France: lat 41--51, lon -5--10)
- **Lambert 93** Easting is typically 100,000--1,300,000 m; Northing is 6,000,000--7,200,000 m
- **UTM** Easting is 100,000--900,000 m; Northing depends on the hemisphere

<Tip>
  If your converted coordinates look wildly wrong (e.g., negative easting, Northing of 0), double-check that you selected the correct source and target CRS. A common mistake is confusing EPSG codes for similar-sounding systems.
</Tip>
